RP2025 - Third time's a charm

Written by Afiya Romain-Bains, October 2025

Representing Physics 2025 was a summer programme for students in Year 11 and Year 12 of Black heritage, organised and delivered by The Blackett Lab Family (BLF) in partnership with The Ogden Trust, Imperial College London, The National Physical Laboratory (NPL), and the Institute of Physics (IOP). This year, we reached around 90 students applied to take part, with 45 students participating across two strands - RP2025 Online, open to students across the UK, and RP2025 London, a three-day in-person experience.

RP2025 Online

Students from across the country joined a day of inspiring talks exploring diverse areas of physics — from optics and quantum technology with Dr Adetunmise Dada (University of Glasgow), to theoretical astrophysics with Jordan Iga (University of Oxford), and neuroimaging with Dr Yolanda Ohene (University of Manchester). The event also featured a careers panel with physicists working in project management, software engineering, patent law, quantum research, and leadership, and a university admissions session led by Dr Mark Richards (Imperial College London).

Impact:

  • Students identifying as a physicist increased from 57% to 70%.
  • 90% found it valuable to hear from Black physicists about their careers.
  • 80% gained greater insight into the UCAS process and next steps.

RP2025 London

The London cohort took part in a three-day in-person experience at Imperial College London, NPL, and the IOP.

📍 Day 1 - Imperial College London

Students built community through icebreakers, attended inspiring talks from Prof Adam Masters and Dr Jess Wade, carried out hands-on experiments in the undergraduate labs, and received university application advice from Dr Mark Richards.

📍 Day 2 - National Physical Laboratory (NPL)

Students toured world-leading physics facilities, designed and launched water rockets, and heard career insights from NPL’s Graduate and Apprenticeship teams.

📍 Day 3 - Institute of Physics (IOP)

Students explored physics identity through art-based research, created and tested their own physics-themed games, and celebrated their achievements at a closing ceremony with certificates, alumni talks, and networking.

Impact:

  • Students identifying as a physicist increased from 38% to 68%.
  • 97% said RP2025 London enhanced their knowledge of physics at university and physics-related careers.
  • 90% said RP2025 London increased their interest in physics.

Summary

The programme aimed to improve perceptions of physics among students of Black heritage and increase the likelihood that they would continue to study physics or a related subject at A-Level or university. To achieve this, RP2025 focused on:

  • Demonstrating that physics is interesting and valuable to society.
  • Showing that studying physics opens doors to a wide variety of careers.
  • Promoting the visibility of Black physicists and strengthening students’ physics identities.
Representing Physics continues to demonstrate the power of representation and community in transforming how young people see their place in physics.
